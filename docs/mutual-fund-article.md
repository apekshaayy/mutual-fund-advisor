# Mutual Funds

## About Mutual Funds

A mutual fund is an investment vehicle that pools money from multiple investors to invest in a diversified portfolio of stocks, government bonds, etc.

- As the number of investments increase (appreciation of investments), the **Net Asset Value (NAV)** increases.
- This increase results in gains and returns for investors.

---

## Fund Manager

A Fund Manager is a professional who invests investors' money across multiple instruments to reduce overall risks.

---

## Choosing The Right Fund

Depends on:

- **Financial goals**
- **Risk appetite:** low / medium / high
- **Investment horizon:** short-term / long-term

## How Mutual Funds Work

### 1. Pooling of Money
Investors contribute money into one pool, giving the fund more buying power than any individual investor. This pooled capital buys a mix of securities per the fund's objectives — allowing small investors to access bigger markets.

### 2. Professional Management
A fund manager runs the fund and allocates money across assets according to the fund type and investment strategy.

### 3. Diversified Investment
The fund spreads money across many securities so that poor performance in one holding does not impact the complete portfolio. Diversification reduces risk and smoothens returns compared to holding a single stock or bond.

### 4. Investor Ownership
Ownership is proportional to the number of units held.

| NAV Direction | Effect on Investor |
|---|---|
| NAV ↑ | More ownership value |
| NAV ↓ | Lesser ownership value |

### 5. Returns to Investors
Investors earn returns through **capital gains** and **dividends or interest income**. Returns may be reinvested (to buy more units) or paid out, depending on the fund's policy.

### 6. Buying and Selling
Units can be bought or sold based on the **daily NAV**. A **Systematic Withdrawal Plan (SWP)** allows withdrawing a fixed amount at regular intervals.

### 7. Fees
| Fee Type | Details |
|---|---|
| **Expense Ratio** | Annual management fee as % of AUM. SEBI cap: 2.25% (equity), 1.75% (debt) |
| **Exit Load** | Fee charged on premature redemption |
| **Transaction Charges** | ₹100–₹150 per transaction above ₹10,000 |
| **Other Charges** | Registrar fees, custodian fees, stamp duty |

### 8. Tax Implications
- Earnings subject to **capital gains tax** — varies by holding period and fund type.
- Dividends are added to income and taxed per your income slab rate.

## Modes of Investment

| Mode | Description |
|---|---|
| **Lump Sum** | Invest a large amount in one go |
| **SIP (Systematic Investment Plan)** | Invest a fixed amount at regular intervals (monthly, quarterly, etc.) |

---

## How to Invest in Mutual Funds

1. Assess your financial goals and risk appetite
2. Open an investment account
3. Complete **KYC** (Know Your Customer)
4. Choose a Mutual Fund Scheme
5. Select investment mode (Lump Sum or SIP)
6. Purchase Mutual Fund units
7. Monitor and review regularly

---

## Calculating Returns

| # | Method | Formula | Used For |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Absolute Returns** | `(Final Value - Initial Investment) / Initial Investment × 100` | General return % |
| 2 | **CAGR / Annualised Returns** | `(Final Value / Initial Investment)^(1/n) - 1` | Lump Sum |
| 3 | **XIRR** (Extended Internal Rate of Return) | Complex formula | SIP |

## Disadvantages of Mutual Funds

- **Exit Load** — penalty for early withdrawal
- **High Cost** — expense ratios eat into returns
- **Over-diversification** — can dilute gains
- **Market Risk** — investments are subject to market fluctuations
